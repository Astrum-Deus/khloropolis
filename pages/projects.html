The Projects

Explore the initiatives and research projects that have been undertaken.

Rooftop Greenhouses

A better alternative of roof top garden, meant for performance and efficiency. These greenhouses are designed to optimize space and resources, providing a controlled environment for year-round cultivation of various crops in urban settings.

Vertical farms and living walls

Vertical farming and living walls are innovative urban agriculture solutions that maximize space by growing plants vertically. These systems utilize hydroponics or aeroponics to cultivate a variety of crops, improving air quality and providing fresh produce in densely populated areas.

Urban Micro-forest

An urban micro-forest is a small, densely planted area of trees and vegetation within a city. These green spaces provide numerous environmental benefits, including improved air quality, reduced urban heat island effect, enhanced biodiversity, and increased community well-being by offering a natural oasis in the midst of urban development.

Projects over the years

2017 | Alley Garden Project (Doh Eain)

The Alley Garden Project, implemented by the urban social enterprise Doh Eain between 2017 and 2019, focused on reclaiming neglected public spaces in Yangon's Central Business District for community food security, native urban vegetation, and public recreation. By successfully clearing and transforming multiple historical 15-foot-wide Back Drainage Spaces behind colonial-era building blocks, the project successfully restored critical urban land while introducing decentralized Bokashi composting bins to process local food scraps directly within the alley ecosystem. Ultimately, over 80 downtown urban households actively participated in sorting household food waste and managing the shared vegetation bins, creating a replicable framework for downtown community-led environmental stewardship.

2021 | Urban Resilience Farming (World Vision Myanmar)

Launched in 2021 by World Vision Myanmar, the Urban Resilience Project focused heavily on enhancing food security, nutritional self-reliance, and livelihood opportunities for vulnerable families living in the high-density industrial margins of Shwepyitha and Hlaingtharyar Townships in Yangon. To address severe space limitations and high economic vulnerability, the initiative established specialized community committees to train hundreds of local residents in small-scale backyard cultivation and household waste management. A core feature of the project involved participatory Urban Asset Mapping exercises, which taught local youth groups to identify underutilized urban spatial patches and water access points for community food production, while simultaneously forming Community-Led Savings Groups to help farming families save income from their home harvests and build micro-businesses.

2022 | Hydroponic Resilience (Habitat for Humanity)

In 2022, Habitat for Humanity Myanmar launched a targeted hydroponics initiative in high-density, vulnerable urban wards across Yangon to provide resource-constrained families and women-led households with a reliable method to grow fresh produce and medicinal herbs amidst severe food price inflation. The project circumvented traditional urban constraints like poor soil quality and lack of ground space by conducting specialized technical workshops that trained 25 local women to build, maintain, and harvest nutrient-film hydroponic systems. These modular, soil-free agricultural setups were specifically configured to sit on roofs, small balconies, or window ledges, successfully removing the need for horizontal ground space or clean topsoil while building long-term climate and economic resilience.
